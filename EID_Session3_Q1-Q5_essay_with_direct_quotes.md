## Q1. You were the senior public health officer in Wuhan in December 2019… What questions? How obtain answers? What measures reduce risk?

Facing a cluster of severe adult pneumonia among people working in a wholesale market, I would manage the event as a potential high-impact respiratory outbreak with serious epidemic potential and treat uncertainty as a reason to accelerate—not delay—action. The first questions are epidemiologic and operational: Are cases linked by time–place–person, and what exposures plausibly connect them (market work, animal contact, supply chains, household clusters)? Most importantly, is there sustained human-to-human transmission and silent spread via asymptomatic or presymptomatic infection, which would immediately raise the risk profile and justify broader control measures (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). In parallel, I would prioritize estimates of key transmission parameters such as the basic reproduction number and the generation interval, because containment difficulty depends strongly on these quantities (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).

To obtain answers quickly, I would broaden early surveillance and case definitions rather than restricting them to a narrow travel/exposure criterion, while simultaneously implementing intensive case finding and contact tracing. Because a large “iceberg” of infections can be undocumented and a material fraction may be asymptomatic, I would interpret negative findings cautiously and build systems that can detect cryptic transmission (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Operationally, I would ensure urgent clinical sampling and timely sharing of clinical, epidemiological, and laboratory data; Singh et al. argue that future outbreaks with respiratory features should be approached with a rebuttable presumption of sustained H2H and asymptomatic transmission, consistent with a precautionary approach (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10).

Measures to reduce risk should be layered. At the individual level: isolate suspected/confirmed cases and ensure the capacity to isolate infectious individuals, which is central to containment (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). At the population level: implement early NPIs as warranted—especially if evidence suggests ongoing transmission—because early NPIs can reduce spread while data and countermeasures mature (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2). At the interface level: reduce exposures associated with spillover risk, consistent with spillover prevention priorities (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). Finally, I would meet IHR duties by rapidly assessing the event and ensuring timely notification and transparent information-sharing through the national focal point, rather than waiting for perfect certainty in a fast-moving respiratory outbreak (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.7).

## Q2. What were failures and successes of interventions at national and international level in containing SARS‑CoV‑2?

The early months of COVID‑19 illustrate how small delays can be amplified by exponential growth. Singh et al. describe that the chronology showed dedication by WHO and some national governments, but also areas that “could have been quicker,” including outbreak notifications under IHR, presumption/confirmation of human-to-human transmission, and the timing of PHEIC-related decisions—alongside the public health response of many national governments as a decisive factor (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.1). A key failure mode in a high-impact respiratory outbreak is treating uncertainty as a reason to wait; a precautionary presumption is more appropriate for rapid, silent spread (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). This matters because early SARS‑CoV‑2 dynamics included \(R_0\) estimates in the 2–4 range and substantial undocumented and asymptomatic infection (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).

At the national level, successes tended to cluster around rapid surveillance, decisive early action, and strong implementation capacity. Koelle et al. note that some countries (e.g., New Zealand and Vietnam) achieved almost complete control of initial spread through rigorous surveillance efforts (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). During the first waves, NPIs were widely used; subsequent analyses found NPIs were effective at flattening the curve and temporarily reducing transmission (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.2).

At the international level, COVID‑19 exposed both the centrality and limits of the IHR framework, prompting reform debates. Habibi et al. note that the 2024 amendments aimed to strengthen implementation, including requiring a National IHR Authority to coordinate national implementation (Habibi et al., 2025, *The 2024 Amendments to the International Health Regulations…*, p.2). The overall lesson is that rapid, precautionary national action aligned with transparent international notification and cooperation is essential.

## Q3. Describe and analyse ecological/environmental/microbial/host factors contributing to emergence and spread of SARS and COVID‑19

Ecological and environmental pressures shape the probability of zoonotic spillover, while microbial and host factors determine how effectively an emergent virus spreads once it enters humans. Vora et al. argue that spillover risk increases when human–animal contact intensifies, including via wildlife trade, animal farming, and land-use change (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). Singh et al. emphasize that IHR mainly structures detection and notification once human cases appear, but contains limited detail on assessing zoonotic risks before human cases and does not strongly address upstream drivers—supporting the need for One Health integration (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.7).

Once spillover has occurred, microbial and host factors become dominant for spread. Koelle et al. stress that containment depends on the ability to isolate infectious individuals, which becomes difficult when infections are asymptomatic or mild (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Over time, variants and immune escape further reshape epidemic trajectories; Omicron’s mutational profile included many mutations predicted to enable immune escape (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.5).

## Q4. How is COVID‑19 transmitted and how can you control the epidemic/pandemic?

COVID‑19 transmission dynamics demand a control strategy that assumes silent spread and acts quickly. Koelle et al. describe how NPIs (lockdowns, distancing, masks) were used to flatten the curve, and explain why this is desirable: preventing health system overload, reducing overshoot, and buying time for better care and therapeutics (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Quantitative analyses found NPIs were effective at flattening the curve and temporarily reducing reproduction numbers (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.2). As the vaccine era began, key questions became durability of protection, variant protection, and transmission reduction (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3).

Because respiratory pathogens can outrun “perfect information,” Singh et al. recommend a precautionary stance for novel respiratory outbreaks: a rebuttable presumption of sustained H2H and asymptomatic transmission, with urgent collection of full early clinical/epidemiological/laboratory data (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10).

## Q5. Discuss parameters to measure COVID‑19 impact; interpret differences between Hong Kong, USA, and New Zealand in 2020–2022

Assessing COVID‑19 impact requires a basket of metrics because any single measure can mislead. Thacker et al. argue that allocation of public health resources should be based on objective assessments of health status and burden, including morbidity, mortality, disability, preventability, and costs (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.1). They also note that core measures available to evaluate population health continue to be morbidity, mortality, and disability (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.2). In particular, premature mortality can be summarized using YPLL, and overall burden can be summarized with DALY, which combines mortality and morbidity measures (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.3; p.5).

Interpreting differences between HK/USA/NZ across 2020–2022 requires a consistent framework: policy and behavior (including NPIs), seasonality, and immunity (including waning) together shape incidence patterns (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3). Early in the pandemic, rigorous surveillance contributed to almost complete initial control in some settings (e.g., New Zealand) (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Over time, vaccination and variant dynamics further changed relationships between cases and severe outcomes.
