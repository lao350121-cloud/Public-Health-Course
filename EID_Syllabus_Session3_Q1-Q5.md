## Q1. (Wuhan, Dec 2019) What questions? How to answer? What measures to reduce risk?

- **Key questions (what you must clarify fast)**: Is there **sustained human-to-human transmission** and **silent/asymptomatic transmission** (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.10)? What is likely **\(R_0\)** and the **generation interval**, which determine containment difficulty (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1)? How large is the **undetected “iceberg”** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1)? Is there a plausible **animal–human interface** (market/wildlife/livestock) driving spillover risk (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2)?
- **How to obtain answers (minimum dataset)**: implement an early **broad case definition** and active case finding (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.10); do rapid descriptive epi (time–place–person), exposure histories, and contact tracing to test for presymptomatic/asymptomatic spread (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1); collect and share clinical/epi/lab data urgently (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.10).
- **Immediate public-health measures**: isolate cases and **build capacity to isolate infectious individuals** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1); initiate NPIs to reduce transmission while evidence accumulates (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2); reduce spillover exposure pathways (e.g., market/wildlife trade risk controls) (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2).
- **IHR duty (do not delay)**: assess urgent events rapidly and **notify WHO of a potential PHEIC within 24h of assessment via the National Focal Point** (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.7; WHO, 2005, *International Health Regulations (2005)*).

## Q2. Failures and successes of national/international interventions in containing SARS‑CoV‑2

- **Where things failed (early phase)**: the COVID-19 chronology highlights delays/insufficiency in **IHR-based outbreak notifications**, in presuming/confirming **human-to-human transmission**, and in timely **PHEIC-related actions**, with national responses often being the biggest determinant of spread (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.1). A key structural friction is that waiting to complete formal notification steps (e.g., Article 6 processes) can be too slow for high-impact respiratory viruses (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.10).
- **Why delays were costly**: early dynamics were compatible with hard-to-contain spread (estimated \(R_0\) **2–4**) plus large undocumented and asymptomatic fractions (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1), meaning “wait for certainty” strategies lose time.
- **What worked**: rapid surveillance and decisive early action—some countries achieved near-complete initial control via **rigorous surveillance efforts** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1), and analyses show **NPIs were effective at flattening the curve** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.2).
- **How systems tried to improve**: post‑COVID reforms aimed to strengthen IHR implementation, including designating a **National IHR Authority** and creating an implementation committee (Habibi et al., 2025, *The 2024 Amendments to the International Health Regulations: A New Era for Global Health Law in Pandemic Preparedness and Response?*, p.2).

## Q3. Ecological/environmental/microbial/host factors driving emergence and spread (SARS & COVID‑19)

- **Ecological/environmental (spillover pressure)**: spillover risk rises with more human–animal contact—**wildlife trade**, **animal farming**, and **deforestation/land-use change** (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). Vora et al. argue effective prevention should prioritize four actions: protect forests, regulate/banning high-risk wildlife trade/markets, improve farm biosecurity, and improve health/economic security in hotspots (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2).
- **Microbial/virological (transmission advantage)**: early SARS‑CoV‑2 spread was consistent with \(R_0\) **2–4**, plus substantial **presymptomatic/asymptomatic transmission**, undermining classic symptom-based containment (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).
- **Host/population factors**: large fractions of mild/asymptomatic infection and “undocumented” infections can mask early spread (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Over time, **variants** and **immune escape** plus **waning** shape epidemic waves (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.5).
- **One Health governance gap**: Singh et al. note IHR mainly addresses detection/notification once human cases exist and is weaker on upstream zoonotic drivers—supporting the need for One Health integration (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.7–9).

## Q4. How is COVID‑19 transmitted, and how can you control an epidemic/pandemic?

- **Transmission features that matter for control**: early estimates \(R_0\) **2–4** and the need to understand the **generation interval** signal high spread potential (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Control is complicated by **presymptomatic** and **asymptomatic** transmission and a large “undocumented” fraction (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).
- **Control logic**:
  - **Isolation & rapid detection**: containment depends on the capacity to **isolate infectious individuals** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).
  - **NPIs**: early NPIs (lockdowns/distancing/masks) can **flatten the curve** to avoid overwhelming health systems and buy time (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2).
  - **Vaccines + variants**: vaccines provided excellent protection against severe outcomes, but strategy must adapt to waning and variant immune escape (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3; Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.5).
- **Precautionary stance for future respiratory threats**: approach novel outbreaks with a **rebuttable presumption** of sustained H2H and asymptomatic transmission, while urgently collecting the full suite of early data (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.10).

## Q5. What parameters measure COVID‑19 impact? Why HK/USA/NZ outcomes differed (2020–2022)?

- **Impact parameters beyond cases/deaths**: allocate and evaluate public-health resources using objective assessments of **morbidity, mortality, disability, preventability, and costs** (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.1). Useful complementary metrics include:
  - **Mortality rates** (overall and age-specific) (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.3).
  - **YPLL (years of potential life lost)** to weight premature deaths (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.3).
  - **DALYs** (integrating mortality + morbidity/disability) (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.3).
- **How to interpret cross-country differences**: “cases per million” depends heavily on detection in a disease with large undocumented/asymptomatic infection (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Outcome differences across 2020–2022 are plausibly driven by:
  - **Timing/strength of surveillance + NPIs** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2) (NZ is cited as achieving near-complete early control via rigorous surveillance) (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).
  - **Vaccination rollout, waning, and variant waves** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3; Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.5), which shift the relationship between cases and deaths over time.
