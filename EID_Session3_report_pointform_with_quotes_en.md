## Q1 (Wuhan, Dec 2019): What questions? How to obtain answers? What measures reduce risk?

- **Point 1: Early case definitions should not be overly narrow (or you will miss spillover/cryptic spread)**
  - **Source**: Singh et al., 2021 — *How an outbreak became a pandemic…* (Lancet), **p.10**
  - **Quote**:
    > inclusive case definition of confirmed and suspected 
    > cases—for example, not restricting the case definition to 
    > travellers from Wuhan—might have resulted in earlier 
    > detection and response in countries, and in potentially 
    > more accurate estimates of the size of the outbreak.

- **Point 2: For novel respiratory outbreaks, assume H2H + possible asymptomatic spread first (precautionary approach), then rapidly test/refute**
  - **Source**: Singh et al., 2021 — *How an outbreak became a pandemic…* (Lancet), **p.10**
  - **Quote**:
    > Future outbreaks of novel diseases with respiratory 
    > features should be approached with a rebuttable 
    > presumption of the risk of sustained human-to-human 
    > and asymptomatic transmission, which is consistent 
    > with a precautionary approach warranted by the potential 
    > scale of harm in not doing so.

- **Point 3: To describe transmission intensity, define \(R_0\) clearly**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > ber R0 (1), defined as the average number of  
    > secondary cases arising from a primary case  
    > in a completely susceptible population (2).

- **Point 4: Early SARS‑CoV‑2 had \(R_0\) estimates ~2–4 (pre-lockdown), implying rapid growth**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > This led to estimates ofR0 ranging  
    > between 2 and 4 before the implementation  
    > of lockdown measures in Wuhan.

- **Point 5: Do not rely only on symptoms—early COVID had substantial undocumented infection and notable asymptomatic infection**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > to infer the proportion of SARS-CoV-2 cases that  
    > went undocumented before 23 January 2020  
    > (yielding an estimate of 86%) (4).  
    > ...  
    > some of the first estimates of the proportion  
    > of infections that remained asymptomatic: 18  
    > to 31% (5, 6).

- **Point 6: What drives spillover risk? Increased opportunities for human–animal contact**
  - **Source**: Vora et al., 2022 — *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers* (Nature), **p.2**
  - **Quote**:
    > The risk of spillover is greater when there are  
    > more opportunities for animals and humans  
    > to make contact, for instance in the trade of  
    > wildlife, in animal farming or when forests are  
    > cleared for mining, farming or roads.

- **Point 7: IHR requirement: if it is a potential PHEIC, notify WHO quickly (within 24 hours of assessment)**
  - **Source**: Singh et al., 2021 — *How an outbreak became a pandemic…* (Lancet), **p.7** (IHR time-period comparison table)
  - **Quote**:
    > State Notify WHO of potential  
    > PHEIC within 24 h of  
    > assessment, via NFP

## Q2: Failures and successes of national/international interventions (why some places lost control vs. contained)

- **Point 1: Reading 1 explicitly states key elements “could have been quicker” (IHR notifications, H2H confirmation, PHEIC, etc.)**
  - **Source**: Singh et al., 2021 — *How an outbreak became a pandemic…* (Lancet), **p.1**

- **Point 2: Success example: New Zealand/Vietnam achieved near-complete initial control via rigorous surveillance**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > Although there were a few countries, such  
    > as New Zealand and Vietnam, that achieved  
    > almost complete control of the initial spread  
    > through rigorous surveillance efforts (11, 12),

- **Point 3: What are NPIs and why do they work? They “flatten the curve”**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > Nonpharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) in-  
    > cluded lockdowns, social distancing measures,  
    > and, eventually, the use of face masks. Adoption  
    > of NPIs during the first wave of the pandemic  
    > served to flatten the curve— that is, to extend  
    > the period over which cases occurred.

- **Point 4: Quantitative evidence: NPIs were effective at flattening the curve and temporarily reducing transmission**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.2**

  - **Source**: Habibi et al., 2025 — *The 2024 Amendments to the International Health Regulations…* (JLME), **p.2**
  - **Quote**:
    > A revised Article 4, for  
    > instance, now requires States Parties to establish or designate a  
    > “National IHR Authority” with the responsibility for coordinating  
    > national implementation.

  - **Source**: Vora et al., 2022 — *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers* (Nature), **p.2**
  - **Quote**:

- **Point 3: Microbial + host features that undermine containment: need to isolate infectious individuals, but asymptomatic/mild infections make this hard**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > BeyondR0, the ability to contain an emerging  
    > pathogen depends on the capacity to isolate  
    > infectious individuals (1). This in turn de-

## Q5: How to measure COVID‑19 impact; why HK/USA/NZ differed (2020–2022)

- **Point 1: Do not rely on cases/deaths alone—use multidimensional burden measures (morbidity, mortality, disability, costs)**
  - **Source**: Thacker et al., 2006 — *Measuring the public’s health* (Public Health Reports), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > Allocation of public health resources should be based, where feasible, on  
    > objective assessments of health status, burden of disease, injury, and disability,  
    > their preventability, and related costs.

- **Point 2: Traditional measures still include morbidity, mortality, disability**
  - **Source**: Thacker et al., 2006 — *Measuring the public’s health* (Public Health Reports), **p.2**
  - **Quote**:
    > measures available to evaluate the health of popula-  
    > tions continue to be morbidity, mortality, and disabil-  
    > ity.

- **Point 3: YPLL as a way to quantify premature mortality**
  - **Source**: Thacker et al., 2006 — *Measuring the public’s health* (Public Health Reports), **p.3**
  - **Quote**:
    > premature mortality measured by years of potential  
    > life lost (YPLL) before age 75 years

- **Point 4: DALY as a summary measure combining mortality + morbidity**
  - **Source**: Thacker et al., 2006 — *Measuring the public’s health* (Public Health Reports), **p.5**
  - **Quote**:
    > Disability-adjusted life years (DALY) is a summary  
    > measure of burden of disease among populations that  
    > combines mortality and morbidity measures.

- **Point 5: Cross-country differences reflect policy + behavior + seasonality + immunity and its waning**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.3**
  - **Quote**:
    > the result of a combination of factors, including  
    > changes in NPIs, behavioral changes, seasonal  
    > changes... and the development of transmission-  
    > reducing immunity through natural infection  
    > followed by its waning.

- **Point 6: Example: NZ early surveillance contributed to better initial control (different outcomes)**
  - **Source**: Koelle et al., 2022 — *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2* (Science), **p.1**
  - **Quote**:
    > New Zealand and Vietnam, that achieved  
    > almost complete control of the initial spread  
    > through rigorous surveillance efforts
