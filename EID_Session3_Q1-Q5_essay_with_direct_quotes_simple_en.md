## Q1. You were the senior public health officer in Wuhan in December 2019… What questions? How obtain answers? What measures reduce risk?

If I saw several severe pneumonia cases in adults linked to a wholesale market, I would treat this as a serious outbreak that could grow fast. I would not wait for perfect proof before acting. The first key question is: **is there human-to-human spread (H2H)**, including silent spread from people with no symptoms yet (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). If H2H is happening, the risk to the public is much higher.

Next, I would try to understand how fast it can spread. I would estimate **\(R_0\)** and the **generation interval** (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Early SARS‑CoV‑2 had \(R_0\) estimates around **2–4** before lockdown, which suggests fast growth (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). I would also assume there may be many hidden infections: early work suggested many infections were undocumented and some were asymptomatic (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). That means “few reported cases” can still hide a bigger outbreak.

To get answers quickly, I would use a **broad case definition** and active case finding, not only a narrow “market travel” rule (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). I would do fast interviews, map exposures, and do contact tracing to see if cases link to each other (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). I would also share key clinical, epi, and lab information quickly (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10).

For control, I would use layered measures: isolate cases and improve the ability to isolate infectious people (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Start NPIs early if needed (masking, distancing, limits on gatherings) to slow spread (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2). Reduce spillover risks where possible by reducing high-risk human–animal contact (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). Under IHR, potential PHEIC events should be notified quickly via the national focal point (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.7).

## Q2. What were failures and successes of interventions at national and international level in containing SARS‑CoV‑2?

Early COVID spread shows that **speed matters**. Singh et al. explain that some parts of the response “could have been quicker,” including IHR notifications, confirming H2H transmission, and PHEIC-related actions (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.1). A common failure is waiting too long for strong proof when a virus can spread silently. That is why Singh et al. support a precautionary approach for future respiratory threats (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10).

Some places did better because they acted early and had strong surveillance. Koelle et al. mention New Zealand and Vietnam as examples that achieved almost complete control of initial spread through rigorous surveillance (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Also, NPIs mattered: NPIs include lockdowns, distancing, and masks, and they helped “flatten the curve” (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Later analyses found NPIs reduced transmission and could temporarily reduce reproduction numbers (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.2).

After COVID, the world tried to improve the IHR system. For example, Habibi et al. describe changes that require a “National IHR Authority” to coordinate national implementation (Habibi et al., 2025, *The 2024 Amendments to the International Health Regulations…*, p.2). Overall, the lesson is: fast national action plus clear international notification and cooperation.

## Q3. Describe and analyse ecological/environmental/microbial/host factors contributing to emergence and spread of SARS and COVID‑19

Think of this in two steps: (1) how a virus jumps from animals to humans (spillover), and (2) how it spreads between humans.

For spillover, Vora et al. explain that risk increases when humans and animals have more chances to meet—through wildlife trade, animal farming, and land-use change like deforestation (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). This is why a One Health view matters.

After the virus enters humans, the virus’s transmission features and human factors drive spread. Koelle et al. explain that containment depends on isolating infectious people, but this is hard when many infections are mild or asymptomatic (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Over time, variants can change the situation. For example, Omicron had many spike mutations that could help immune escape (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.5).

Also, Singh et al. point out a system issue: the IHR focuses on detection and notification once human cases exist, and it has limited detail on upstream zoonotic risk before human cases (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.7). So prevention must involve One Health work beyond only human health.

## Q4. How is COVID‑19 transmitted and how can you control the epidemic/pandemic?

COVID spreads well, including from people with mild or no symptoms. This makes late, symptom-only control too weak. So control needs layers: isolation, NPIs, and later vaccines.

Koelle et al. explain why “flattening the curve” is important: it prevents hospitals from being overwhelmed, slows the outbreak, and buys time to improve care and test treatments (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). They also report that analyses found NPIs reduced spread and could temporarily reduce reproduction numbers (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.2). When vaccines arrived in late 2020, new questions became key: how long protection lasts, whether vaccines protect against variants, and whether they reduce transmission (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3).

Singh et al. add a clear policy lesson for future outbreaks: for novel respiratory diseases, start with a rebuttable presumption of sustained H2H and asymptomatic transmission (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). In simple terms: act early, then quickly collect evidence to confirm or refute.

## Q5. Discuss parameters to measure COVID‑19 impact; interpret differences between Hong Kong, USA, and New Zealand in 2020–2022

To measure COVID impact, do not use only “cases” and “deaths.” Thacker et al. say public health decisions should use objective measures of burden: morbidity, mortality, disability, and costs (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.1). They also note that common population health measures still include morbidity, mortality, and disability (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.2). For COVID, it helps to add measures like **YPLL** (premature deaths) and **DALY** (overall burden combining mortality and morbidity) (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.3; p.5).

To explain differences between Hong Kong, the USA, and New Zealand over 2020–2022, use a simple frame: policy and behavior (like NPIs), seasonal changes, and immunity (including waning) can all change infection levels (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3). Early on, some places (e.g., New Zealand) had near-complete initial control due to rigorous surveillance (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Later, vaccines and variants changed the link between cases and severe outcomes.
