## Q1. You were the senior public health officer in Wuhan in December 2019… What questions? How obtain answers? What measures reduce risk?

Facing a cluster of severe adult pneumonia among people working in a wholesale market, I would manage the event as a potential high-impact respiratory outbreak with serious epidemic potential and treat uncertainty as a reason to accelerate—not delay—action. The first questions are epidemiologic and operational: Are cases linked by time–place–person, and what exposures plausibly connect them (market work, animal contact, supply chains, household clusters)? Most importantly, is there sustained human-to-human transmission and silent spread via asymptomatic or presymptomatic infection, which would immediately raise the risk profile and justify broader control measures (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic: a chronological analysis of crucial junctures and international obligations in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic*, p.10). In parallel, I would prioritize estimates of key transmission parameters such as the basic reproduction number and the generation interval, because containment difficulty depends strongly on these quantities (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Early COVID-19 analyses highlighted how estimates of \(R_0\) depend on generation-interval assumptions, and why rapid collection of high-resolution outbreak datasets matters (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).

To obtain answers quickly, I would broaden early surveillance and case definitions rather than restricting them to a narrow travel/exposure criterion, while simultaneously implementing intensive case finding and contact tracing. Because a large “iceberg” of infections can be undocumented and a material fraction may be asymptomatic, I would interpret negative findings cautiously and build systems that can detect cryptic transmission (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Operationally, I would ensure urgent clinical sampling and timely sharing of clinical, epidemiological, and laboratory data; Singh et al. argue that future outbreaks with respiratory features should be approached with a rebuttable presumption of sustained H2H and asymptomatic transmission, consistent with a precautionary approach (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10).

Measures to reduce risk should be layered. At the individual level: isolate suspected/confirmed cases and ensure the capacity to isolate infectious individuals, which is central to containment (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). At the population level: implement early nonpharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) as warranted—especially if evidence suggests ongoing transmission—because early NPIs can reduce spread while data and countermeasures mature (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2). At the interface level: reduce exposures associated with spillover risk (e.g., controls on high-risk wildlife trade/markets and animal farming biosecurity), consistent with spillover prevention priorities (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). Finally, I would meet IHR duties by rapidly assessing the event and ensuring timely notification and transparent information-sharing through the national focal point, rather than waiting for perfect certainty in a fast-moving respiratory outbreak (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.7; WHO, 2005, *International Health Regulations (2005)*).

## Q2. What were failures and successes of interventions at national and international level in containing SARS‑CoV‑2?

The early months of COVID-19 illustrate how small delays can be amplified by exponential growth. Singh et al. describe that the chronology showed dedication by WHO and some national governments, but also areas that “could have been quicker,” including outbreak notifications under IHR, presumption/confirmation of human-to-human transmission, and the timing of PHEIC-related decisions—alongside the public health response of many national governments as a decisive factor (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.1). A key failure mode in a high-impact respiratory outbreak is treating uncertainty as a reason to wait. Singh et al. argue that early definitions and surveillance did not adequately factor in possible human-to-human transmission and silent/asymptomatic spread, and they recommend a precautionary “rebuttable presumption” for future respiratory threats (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). This matters because early SARS‑CoV‑2 dynamics included \(R_0\) estimates in the 2–4 range and substantial undocumented infection and asymptomatic infection, making containment difficult if actions are triggered only after clear symptomatic signals appear (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1).

At the national level, successes tended to cluster around rapid surveillance, decisive early action, and strong implementation capacity. Koelle et al. note that some countries (e.g., New Zealand and Vietnam) achieved almost complete control of initial spread through rigorous surveillance efforts, while many other countries experienced rapid takeoff of community transmission (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). During the first waves, NPIs such as lockdowns and distancing were implemented widely; subsequent quantitative analyses found NPIs were effective at flattening the curve and temporarily reducing reproduction numbers (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.2). These successes reflect the logic of “buying time” for health systems and for the development of better clinical strategies and countermeasures.

At the international level, COVID-19 exposed both the centrality and limits of the IHR framework, prompting reform debates. Habibi et al. note that the 2024 amendment package aimed to incrementally strengthen IHR implementation and integrate equity across provisions, including mechanisms such as a National IHR Authority and new implementation-focused bodies (Habibi et al., 2025, *The 2024 Amendments to the International Health Regulations: A New Era for Global Health Law in Pandemic Preparedness and Response?*, p.2). Taken together, the main lesson is that rapid, precautionary national action aligned with transparent international notification and cooperation is essential; “containment” is less a single measure than a coordinated system that must move at the speed of the pathogen.

## Q3. Describe and analyse ecological/environmental/microbial/host factors contributing to emergence and spread of SARS and COVID‑19

Ecological and environmental pressures shape the probability of zoonotic spillover, while microbial and host factors determine how effectively an emergent virus spreads once it enters humans. Vora et al. argue that spillover risk increases when human–animal contact intensifies, including via wildlife trade, animal farming, and land-use change (especially deforestation); they propose four major actions to reduce spillover risk: protect tropical/subtropical forests, regulate/ban high-risk live wildlife markets and trade, improve biosecurity and surveillance in farmed animals, and improve health and economic security in emergence hotspots (Vora et al., 2022, *Want to prevent pandemics? Stop spillovers*, p.2). These drivers are consistent with a One Health framing in which upstream environmental change can increase opportunities for pathogen exchange between animals and humans.

Singh et al. emphasize that although the IHR specify core capacities for detection and notification once human cases appear, they provide limited detail on assessing zoonotic risks before human cases and do not create strong obligations to address upstream drivers; they describe fragmented governance across human and animal health sectors and call for coordinated, evidence-based international governance for zoonotic risk that precedes detection in humans (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.8–9). In practical terms, this highlights why spillover prevention and integrated surveillance are not “optional add-ons” but part of preparedness.

Once spillover has occurred, microbial and host factors become dominant for spread. Koelle et al. explain that early assessments focused on \(R_0\) and the generation interval; for SARS‑CoV‑2, early estimates of \(R_0\) ranged between 2 and 4 before Wuhan lockdown, and estimation was complicated by uncertainty in the generation interval (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Critically, containment depended on the ability to isolate infectious individuals, but SARS‑CoV‑2 exhibited substantial asymptomatic and presymptomatic transmission and a high proportion of undocumented infections, undermining purely symptom-triggered interventions (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Over time, viral evolution (variants) and host immunity (infection- and vaccine-derived, followed by waning) further shaped the epidemic trajectory and the shifting relationship between cases and severe outcomes (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3; p.5). In short, emergence is driven by ecological interfaces; pandemic spread is driven by transmission biology interacting with population susceptibility and response capacity.

## Q4. How is COVID‑19 transmitted and how can you control the epidemic/pandemic?

COVID‑19 transmission dynamics demand a control strategy that assumes silent spread and acts quickly. Koelle et al. describe how early containment assessments relied on estimating \(R_0\) and the generation interval, with early \(R_0\) estimates of about 2–4 in Wuhan prior to lockdown and strong dependence on how the generation interval was parameterized (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). They also stress that containment depends on the capacity to isolate infectious individuals, which is challenging when a substantial fraction of infections are asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic and when presymptomatic transmission is frequent (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). This combination makes late, symptom-based responses insufficient.

Control therefore requires layered interventions. First, strengthen detection and isolation capacity through testing, isolation support, rapid contact tracing, and surveillance systems designed to detect community spread—not only imported cases or a narrow exposure category. Second, apply NPIs early when incidence is rising: Koelle et al. describe NPIs (lockdowns, distancing, masks) as central to “flattening the curve,” extending cases over time to avoid overwhelming health systems, reducing overshoot after peaks, and buying time to improve care and evaluate therapeutics (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2). Third, implement vaccination and adapt strategy as evidence evolves: by late 2020, vaccines were developed rapidly and showed excellent protection against severe outcomes, while subsequent work addressed duration of protection, variant protection, and transmission effects (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3).

Because respiratory pathogens can outrun “perfect information,” Singh et al. recommend a precautionary stance: future novel respiratory outbreaks should be approached with a rebuttable presumption of sustained H2H and asymptomatic transmission and with urgent collection of the full suite of early clinical/epidemiological/laboratory data (Singh et al., 2021, *How an outbreak became a pandemic…*, p.10). In practice, that means acting early with scalable measures, then refining as transmission routes, burden, and variant dynamics become clearer.

## Q5. Discuss parameters to measure COVID‑19 impact; interpret differences between Hong Kong, USA, and New Zealand in 2020–2022

Assessing COVID‑19 impact requires a basket of metrics because any single measure can mislead. Thacker et al. argue that allocation of public health resources should be based on objective assessments of health status and burden, including morbidity, mortality, disability, preventability, and costs, and they caution that measurement choices reflect values and can shift perceived priorities (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.1). Mortality rates (including age-specific rates) remain foundational, but they do not capture how deaths are distributed across age and vulnerability. To address this, Thacker et al. discuss premature mortality as years of potential life lost (YPLL), which weights deaths at younger ages more heavily, and they present disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) as a summary measure combining mortality and morbidity/disability (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.3). These metrics help interpret why countries with similar “deaths per million” might still differ meaningfully in societal loss and long-term burden.

For country comparisons like Hong Kong, the USA, and New Zealand, interpretation must also consider how “cases per million” depends on detection effort in a disease with substantial undocumented infection and asymptomatic infection (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1). Differences in 2020 largely reflect the timing and intensity of surveillance and NPIs; Koelle et al. note New Zealand achieved almost complete control of initial spread through rigorous surveillance, while many settings saw rapid community transmission and then relied on NPIs to flatten the curve (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.1–2). In 2021–2022, the relationship between infections and deaths shifted under vaccination, waning, and variant waves: vaccines showed excellent protection against severe outcomes, but questions of durability, variant immune escape, and transmission reduction became central for predicting longer-term dynamics (Koelle et al., 2022, *The changing epidemiology of SARS-CoV-2*, p.3; p.5). Thus, to explain differing outcomes across these countries and years, one should triangulate: (1) epidemic timing and policy intensity (NPIs/surveillance), (2) vaccination coverage and protection over time, (3) variant timing, and (4) burden metrics beyond raw deaths—especially YPLL, DALYs, and cost—so that “impact” is measured as health loss, not just case counts (Thacker et al., 2006, *Measuring the public’s health*, p.1; p.3).
